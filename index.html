Melting Ice with Heat Rays

Summary: Our objective is to produce a rudimentary heat transfer simulation, whereby heat will be applied to a solid object and the interactions between the constituent particles as they undergo phase change will be observed. The heat is applied in the form of rays cast onto the object, and upon ray-particle intersection, the ray will transfer its heat to the particle. Once a particle has gathered enough heat to undergo phase change, the properties of the particle will be changed from a solid to a liquid, which will be reflected through a simple fluid simulation.

Team Members: Alex Gao, Daniel Hsu, Grace Liu, and Joseph Liu

Problem Description

In reality, we observe three states of matter–solid, liquid, and gas–each with their own distinct properties. Matter can also change states depending on the amount of heat contained by it, which in turn varies its properties. Our objective is to model the process of a solid undergoing phase change and becoming a liquid as it is exposed to a direct heat source. We will model the solid as a point mass system of particles in a spring mesh (similar to the cloth simulation project, except 3D). Each particle will be able to hold heat, which will dynamically vary properties like spring constant that affects its interaction with its surrounding particles. These properties vary with a particle’s own heat as well as the heat possessed by its surrounding particles. Heat is represented as heat rays; just as light rays carry radiance, we will model the heat rays to carry heat and perform the necessary heat transfer calculations to determine how the heat from the heat ray dissipates onto into the particle, and whether it refracts/reflects off a particle to dissipate its heat into surrounding particles as well. Once the particle reaches a critical point (melting point), its properties will undergo a larger, more significant change as its phase changes from a solid state to a liquid state. Specifically, it will be able to easily detach itself from the solid to form droplets of liquid subject to forces like gravity and cohesion (allowing it to bond with solid surfaces and other particles of liquid).

Goals and Deliverables

Our demo will allow us to visualize water falling off a block of ice as the block gets melted. We’ll be able to define where heat rays are applied to our object by adjusting factors such as the angle, size of the ray, and intensity of the ray. We will also be able to capture a few images, namely the various stages of the heat transfer interaction as the ice begins to heat up, melt, and transform bit by bit into falling water. If time permits, we will change the block of ice to a different material.

One of our metrics for success will be based on how accurately it corresponds to the most accurate heat transfer simulation on the market to date. The closer our results are, the better. Another metric is how smoothly our program runs in real time on our computers. This is not intended to be a simulation that is indistinguishable from reality, it is meant to be a middle ground between accuracy and performance. If time permits, we can strive to measure our success on how many different types of material we can generate (e.g., butter, mercury, etc.).

In our analysis, we plan to answer the questions below.

  • How do we accurately model how the properties of matter vary with the heat it contains?
  • How do we model the transition of a matter as it changes from a solid to a liquid phase?
  • How do we accurately simulate the behavior of liquids?
  • What methods can we use to make our simulations run efficiently?
  • Is it possible to simplify thermal conduction to PointMasses and still have it behave realistically?
